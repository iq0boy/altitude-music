Universal Audio's Native Manley VOXBOX isn't the channel-strip plug-in you hear about most day to day. For vocals, most engineers naturally reach for the classic SSL 4000 or the Neve 1073. But for us, integrating the native version completely changed our studio workflow.

## The sound of the VOXBOX

The Manley VOXBOX is modeled on a legendary high-end tube machine. What that gives you in practice: a silky coloration and an ultra-musical optical compression that sits before the equalizer. It's a different philosophy, but a formidable one.

On the modern vocals we process at the studio, this plug-in brings a high-end presence and an instant velvety texture. Sibilance is handled gently thanks to the built-in de-esser/limiter that acts exactly where needed, without ever choking the dynamics or making the mix harsh.

## Why not an SSL or Neve strip?

SSL or Neve console emulations are excellent for adding punch, drive and a very incisive edge. That's perfect for some tracks, but on urban or current-pop vocals that need both thickness and air, they can quickly sound too aggressive or cold. The VOXBOX, on the other hand, wraps the signal in a warmth and a "glaze" that's typically high-end and far more forgiving.

## What it changed in practice

Our mixing sessions have become much smoother. We spend far less time stacking four or five different plug-ins (a preamp, two compressors, an EQ, a de-esser) to sculpt a lead vocal. Just by opening the VOXBOX, we get a complete, coherent chain that's devastatingly effective in only a few tweaks.

It's the most impactful software investment we've made on our vocal buses in recent months.
